Victoria Neises, PhD

Marine Mammal Physiologist and Quantitative Ecologist

I am a marine mammal physiologist that uses quantitative analysis to study energetics and nutrition in order to characterize and assess behavioral and physiological impacts and limitations due to environmental change and interactions.

Research

My research is focused on physiology, specifically metabolism and energetics. I am particularly interested in how energetics limit the behavior of an organism, and how organisms respond to physiological limitations caused by environmental changes. In particular, my work has focused on Alaskan ecosystems and has attempted to explore how changes in the prey field might be impacting pinniped populations (seals and sea lions) on a physiological level.

To assess these types of questions I use various quantitative methods, including nonparametric analysis, such as PERMANOVA, linear discriminant analysis (LDA), and non-metric multidimensional scaling (NMDS), as well as regression analyses like GLM, GAM, GLMM, and GAMM, and machine learning techniques such as random forest analysis.

My research interests include nutritional and metabolic physiology, quantitative ecology, and foraging behavior.

Respiratory Physiology

My Masters research was a collaboration with Moss Landing Marine Laboratories examining how sea lions physiologically respond to increased foraging stress caused by changes in their prey field. In this study we determined that while sea lions are able to adjust their dive behavior to maintain foraging efficiency as the cost of foraging increases, their ability to sequester accumulating carbon dioxide diminishes. We proposed that carbon dioxide may be a more sensitive physiological marker than oxygen when examining the metabolic cost of foraging.

Energetics and Nutritional Physiology

My dissertation research was a collaboration with Alaska Department of Fish and Game focused on the examination of blubber fatty acids of Alaskan harbor seals and Steller sea lions and included two large multi-decadal data sets. With these data sets I was able to investigate regional, seasonal, and age class differences in blubber fatty acids of harbor seals, as well as examine the blubber fatty acids of lactating harbor seals, and how they differ to non-lactating harbor seals and other lactating phocids and otariids. I was also able to compare blubber fatty acids of Alaskan harbor seal and Steller sea lions in the same regions during the same spatial and temporal timeframes to determine if differences in foraging strategies were reflected in their blubber fatty acid profiles. Because both mammal species are resident species to the areas they inhabit and are easier to study than fish or invertebrate species in the area due to their size, they are seen as health indicators for the ecosystems they inhabit. Understanding how their diet is changing gives management agencies insight into how the fish and invertebrate communities are changing, providing an indirect way to measure ecosystem health.
