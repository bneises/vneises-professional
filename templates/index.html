Research

To date, my work has focused on marine mammals in Alaskan ecosystems and has attempted to explore how changes in the prey field might be impacting pinniped (seals and sea lions) populations from both a metabolic perspective, as well as a nutritional and digestive standpoint.

To assess these types of questions I use a variety of quantitative methods, including nonparametric analysis, such as PERMANOVA, linear discriminant analysis (LDA), and non-metric multidimensional scaling (NMDS), as well as regression analyses like GLM, GAM, GLMM, and GAMM, as well as machine learning techniques such as random forest analysis.

Nutritional Physiology

My work in nutritional physiology focuses on the examination of blubber fatty acid profiles of Alaskan harbor seals and Steller sea lions. Because blubber fatty acids are largely derived from diet in these animals, we are able to use them as a proxy for diet and examine how they differ and change over time. In this study, I was able to use two large large multi-decadal data sets to examine spatial and temporal differences in blubber fatty acid profiles of Alaskan harbor seal populations, examine blubber fatty acid profiles among pregnant and lactating harbor seals, and mother pup pairs, and lastly, compare regional and seasonal blubber fatty acid profiles of Alaskan harbor seals and Steller sea lions to examine the degree of niche partitioning within those regions. Because both mammal species are resident species to the areas they inhabit and are easier to study than fish or invertebrate species in the area due to their size, they are seen as health indicators for the ecosystems they inhabit. Understanding how their diet is changing gives management agencies insight into how the fish and invertebrate communities are changing, providing an indirect way to measure ecosystem health. This project was a collaboration between Baylor University and Alaska Department of ish and Game.

Respiratory Physiology

My work in respiratory physiology focused on how sea lions physiologically respond to increased foraging stress caused by changes in their prey field. In this study we determined that while sea lions are able to adjust their dive behavior to maintain foraging efficiency as the cost of foraging increases, their ability to sequester accumulating carbon dioxide diminishes. We proposed that carbon dioxide may be a more sensitive physiological marker than oxygen when examining the metabolic cost of foraging. This project was a collaboration between Alaska Pacific University and Moss Landing Marine Laboratories.
